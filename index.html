Ada Lovelace

Analyst, Metaphysician and Founder of Scientific Computing

Ada Lovelace was born Augusta Ada Byon in London on December 10, 1815. She was the daughter of Annabella Byron and Lord Byron, the famous poet. Only a month after Ada was born, her mother left Lord Byron due to his alcholism and infidelity. Shortly after their seperation, Lord Byron would leave England forever. Ada never knew him personally.

From an early age, Ada showed a talent for numbers and language. And Lady Byron, determined to prevent her daughter from inheriting her father's volatile temperament, promoted Ada's interest in mathematics, science and logic.

Although a sickly child, Ada pursued her education tirelessly. Throughout her lifetime she woulc receive instruction from Augustus De Morgan, the first professor of mathematics at the University of London; William Frend, a social reformer; and eventually Charles Babbage, a mathematician and inventor who would later be referred to as the father of the computer. She also, through her educational exploits, would meet famed scientists such as Michael Faraday, Andrew Crosse and Charles Wheatstone.

In 1835, at the age of 19, Ada was married to aristocrat William King. When he was made Earl of Lovelace in 1838 she officially became Lady Ada King, Countess of Lovelace. This is why she is generally called Ada Lovelace. Her and King had three children.

Ada was introduced to Charles Babbage by their mutual friend and her tutor Mary Somerville in 1833. She was particularly interested in Babbage's work on building an Analytical Engine, whose design had all the essential elements of a modern-day computer. Lovelace actually translated an article, which described the Analytical Engine, by an Italian mathematician named Luigi Menabrea, for publication in England.

Babbage eventually asked Ada to expand the article, “as she understood the machine so well”. Her final article ended up being three times longer than the original and included several early ‘computer programs’ as well as detailed and astute observations on the potential uses of the machine, such as the creation of music. “The Analytical Engine,” she said, “weaves algebraic patterns, just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves.”

Babbage would go on to only build a small part of the Analytical Engine and it would never be fully finished until Ada’s notes became one of the critical documents to inspire Alan Turing’s work on the first modern computers in the 1940s. In honor of Ada Lovelace's work, the early programming language Ada was named after her. And she is still hailed today as a powerful symbol for modern women in technology.

Ada Lovelace died of uterine cancer on November 27, 1852, at the age of thirty-six, only a few short years after the publication of “Sketch of the Analytical Engine, with Notes from the Translator”.
